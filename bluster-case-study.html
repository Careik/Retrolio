What is Bluster?

Social media has pivoted from what it was created for and become a place of hatred and unwanted opinions. It has gotten so bad that people are completely ridding social media from their lives.

I believe that there is a way to make social media great and Bluster is the answer to that.

Problem:

With the creation of mySpace social media took off. It began as a place where friends could meet up virtually, and share their lives with one another. It was legitimately a great idea that had good intentions. However, today’s social media is much different than the initial concept. Today you are flooded with people’s opinions, friend requests from strangers, and of course the “perfect lifestyle” leaving you feeling inadequate. People are tired of what today's social media platforms have to offer. Bluster is a new take on social media bringing the space back to its original glory.

Solution:

The goal of Bluster is simple - send broadcasts to your group of friends and award those friends for their achievements.

Users start by creating unique groups with their close friends. This way when you post to a specific group, it is relevant to people who are actually curious about it. There is no more hearing about Aunt Martha’s political views while checking up to see how your best friend is doing. This is social media the way it was intended.

Research

User Survey:

I began with a user survey that was sent out by email. I had over 30 responses and the following results were gathered:

  • 93.1% of participants use social media. Of that group 86.2% say they use social media every day.
  • 60.0% of people said that comments on posts or awards are fairly to very important
  • When asked if people would rather award accolades or points to their friends, 73.3% of participants preferred accolades.
  • Participants were asked if they would like their award to go to a vote or go through immediately, and 66.7% preferred immediately.

I realized quite a few important things through this survey. It was clear that people are tired of social media and the negativity that goes along with it. Also, people want an award system with friends. I initially tossed over the idea of a point based system; however, it was made clear that accolades were preferred.

Competitive Analysis:

In order to understand what some social networking applications are doing right, competitive analysis was performed on Snapchat, Twitter, and Reddit. Snapchat was chosen because it is an app primarily used between true friends, and not just a numbers game. Twitter was chosen for the way users can put out tweets to their friends. Lastly, Reddit was used because they have an award system built into their application. I wanted to look into the specific flowpath users had to go through in order to award posts.

Visual Design

Branding:

Branding, in my opinion, was one of the most important things about this application. It had to feel like an application that a diverse group of people needed to feel comfortable using. I did not want to come off too childish or too professional limiting the audience in any way.

To get started, I created a moodboard to get an idea of what direction I wanted to take. I then explored those ideas even further in order to create the color palette that was used.

Color Palette:

I chose a color palette that is relaxing and that I believe expresses the idea of the brand. This is an application that deals with your closest friends and awarding those friends for their actions. Whether you award these accolades sarcastically or because you want to be nice is up to the user. The idea though stays the same. You are playing with only your closest friends. That is why I chose a dark blue, orange and a magenta as the main colors. The blue shows trust and loyalty as you will be playing with close friends. The orange expresses creativity. The main goal of this application is to give awards for whatever the user sees fit, and there is no doubt that there will be some creative reasons. Lastly, the magenta rounds it out with compassion and a sense of respect. At the end of the days these are your best friends and you will be sharing moments with one another over a period of time. It gives the user an opportunity to look back at those moments and remember past events you may have forgotten about.

typography:

I have also chosen two different fonts that I believe fit the brand and the mission at hand. I wanted an easily legible font for the main typeface; however, I wanted a more easy-going, fun font for some titles and subheaders. I found the font pair of Oxygen and Quicksand to be a great combo that checked all of my boxes.

Application name and logo design process:

It took me a while to figure out what I wanted to name the application. I wanted something short and memorable, and most of all not taken on the app store. That is when I came across “Bluster.” The definition of Bluster is, “ to talk in a loud, aggressive, or indignant way with little effect.”

I thought this was perfect as you are playing with friends and awarding them based on their actions. Whether these awards are in good faith or bad faith does not matter because these are your closest friends. In the end their words have little effect and you understand their motives. People have also told me that it sounds like a combination of the phrase “Ball-buster” but I promise that was not the reason (even though it is quite funny).

For the logo design I began by drawing out as many designs as possible. These ranged from birds talking over one another, to B’s shaped as microphones, all the way to the final design of the B.  This was arguably one of the hardest parts for me because I felt like the logo was so important. I wanted it to have meaning, but also look professional. My thought process behind the final design is simple. The B is created with two “Broadcast” icons. It is supposed to look like two people sending out a broadcast with one slightly overlapping the other. To bring it all together, I colored it with colors from the color palette. Overall I am very happy with how the icon turned out and I believe gives Bluster a strong identity.

User Testing Round 1:

I performed a general round of using testing using the low fidelity wireframes as a paper prototype. The goal was to determine if the layout made sense before I moved on to high fidelity mockups. The biggest improvement that came out of this  was the removal of the bottom tab bar between the “Feed Overview” and the “Friend’s List.” It was determined that once users created their initial groups, the need to go to the friends greatly decreased. This opened up a lot more space on the main screen and increased the overall feel of the application.

High Fidelity Testing:

During this phase, I went through three rounds of user testing, each round building upon the last. Each user test brought valuable information that helped shape the final design of Bluster. The tasks given to the testers were as follows:

  • Sign in.
  • Navigate to their friends list and accept the friend invite.
  • Enter into their “Apartment Circle” and respond to Jonathan B’s most recent broadcast.
  • Post an award to a friend.
  • Create a group.
  • Logout.

Below are the major changes and the reasons for those changes.

Active and Inactive Buttons

In my initial mockups, my inactive and active buttons were too similar. This led to users not noticing the changes, thus they thought they were doing something incorrect. I completely overhauled the buttons to create greater contrast which proved to help in later testing.

Awarding Accolades to Friends

I noticed that people sometimes would use the dedicated “award” button, but then sometimes people would go into the user settings and try to click on the member they wanted to award. I created a second path in which a user can take to award their friends. This creates the freedom for users to award their friends through different pathways, which enhances their user experience.

Feed Background Color

I originally was using a brand color as the feed background. This looked good to me, but it clearly was causing contrast issues with users. The button to award or broadcast was getting lost in the background of the feed causing users to look right over it. Once it was fixed users did not struggle finding it anymore.

Logout Location

The last big issue, and arguably the hardest issue to solve was the location of the logout button. It seemed that every single user had a different idea of where this button should be tucked away. I originally had it in settings, but then a user clicked on profile to logout. Logically this makes sense so I moved it into the profile settings and thought that would solve the issue. In the following user test, the user went into general settings rather than their profile settings. The next person thought that it should be in the sidebar navigation and not in profile or settings. Essentially, every person had a different idea of where this button should go.

I specifically asked users after the test if they knew exactly where the logout was on their favorite social media platform. Funny enough, every single one said they did not becasue they have never tried to log out before. This leads me to believe that users just do not know where they should expect the logout.

My final decision was to leave it in the profile settings. Logically, I believe this makes the most sense and users will be famililar with the logout location after their first attempt to log out.

Conclusion

This project was a ton of fun to work on. As someone who used to enjoy social media and now cannot stand it, it was fun for me to try and come up with a solution to make it fun again. The survey that I started this project with helped shape the entire scope of the project. People want to enjoy social media, but today’s options are far too bloated with features and content. People clearly want to create memories with small groups of friends, not their hundreds of “friends” on other platforms. On top of that, people want to promote conversation by awarding their friends for a plethora of reasons. Some people want to be sarcastic, while some want to be genuinely kind. The beauty of it though is that you are with your true friends. Bluster creates a platform to do just that, and I believe is the solution to make social media fun again.

Future Improvements:

If given more time, I would love to do even more user testing. I think that in my survey people really enjoyed the idea of awarding their friends accolades. However, I would love to see how often that feature is used comparatively to something like a broadcast. Also, it would be interesting to look into if an API could be used for GIF functionality. At this moment all the accolades were created by me, but if users had an unlimited amount of accolades it could potentially create greater interest in the accolade concept.

Final Thoughts:

Bluster is an application that makes people think differently about the way they use social media. In recent times, people have grown more and more tired of the tech giants. Between the negativity, privacy concerns, and self-image issues, it makes sense why people are looking for something different.

From the survey I learned that what people crave most in social media is multiple unique groups, sharing updates with friends, awarding friend, and privacy

Bluster has all of these features to provide users with the best social media experience possible. Users can create private circles with their groups of friends. Users can broadcast out to those groups, and users can award their friends within those groups. Bluster is a brand new experience that will change the way people think about social media.

As I said in the beginning, “social media is lackluster. So why not Bluster.”
